Discussion Name:
Should a license be required in order to have a child (procreate)?

1. Thesis:
A license should be required in order to have a child (procreate).

  1.1. Con:
  The practicalities of implementing such a policy would make it incredibly difficult, if not impossible, to implement in most countries.

    1.1.1. Pro:
    Unexpected pregnancies will still occur.

      1.1.1.1. Pro:
      Many parents who unintentionally become pregnant in the status quo are able to keep custody of the child should they decide to not get an abortion. Without a parenting license, many of these people would be at a greater risk of losing custody.

      1.1.1.2. Con:
      Not all parents who become pregnant unexpectedly will fail to acquire a license.

      1.1.1.3. Pro:
      A significant proportion of the pregnancies worldwide are unplanned.

        1.1.1.3.1. Pro:
        In 2013, it was estimated that one in six pregnancies among women in Britain are unplanned, and one in sixty [1] women (1.5%) experience an unplanned pregnancy in a year. This is a huge number of children the state would have to take away.

        1.1.1.3.2. Pro:
        In the US in 2010, more than half of all pregnancies in 28 states were unintentional [2] and the lowest rate for any state was 36%.

        1.1.1.3.3. Pro:
        Almost half of all pregnancies worldwide are unplanned (p. 1 [3]). Given this, to require a license to procreate would mean total prevention of pregnancy in the first instance which, in turn, interferes with individuals' bodily autonomy.

        1.1.1.3.4. Con:
        Better family planning education, as would stem from a parenting licensing system, would help reduce the number of unplanned pregnancies worldwide (p. 13 [4]).

        1.1.1.3.5. Con:
        Even though unplanned pregnancy rates may be high in some regions, most pregnancies in the developed world [5] are still planned.

    1.1.2. Con:
    Governments have the capacity to overcome the practical difficulties of such a policy.

      1.1.2.1. Con:
      Public bodies have a proven record of bureaucratic inefficiency, possibly due to their noncompetitive status (p. 1 [6]).

        1.1.2.1.1. Pro:
        Given the sheer volume of bureaucracy that assessing the global rate of 267 births/minute [7] would entail, this policy could leave a significant backlog of work, rendering it untenable.

          1.1.2.1.1.1. Pro:
          Much like in the fostering assessment process, individual screeners would likely be given an overwhelming workload that would impinge on their ability to assess accurately.

            1.1.2.1.1.1.1. Pro:
            Overloaded case workers [8] can mean flaws and oversight in the screening processes of prospective foster parents and their homes. This can lead to children being placed within foster homes and families that are unsafe or problematic.

            1.1.2.1.1.1.2. Con:
            Digitising [9] the process and tracking of individual assessments could redude screeners' workloads.

        1.1.2.1.2. Con:
        There a a number of effective solutions [10] governments can take to iron out the problems associated with bureaucracy.

          1.1.2.1.2.1. Pro:
          Digitising tasks and records can reduce the burden on potentially overworked staff as well as reducing adminitrative errors (p. 22 [11]).

      1.1.2.2. Pro:
      Governments would have the breadth of knowledge required to assess individuals.

        1.1.2.2.1. Con:
        Around a quarter [12] of children under five across the world have never had their births registered. As such, many countries will simply not have the data they would need in order to enforce this policy.

        1.1.2.2.2. Pro:
        A parenting license would require prospective parents to meet certain criteria before being approved.

          1.1.2.2.2.1. Pro:
          Parenting standards and potential are scrutinised when assessing the suitability of adoptive parents and foster carers. A parenting license assessment could be conducted on similar merits.

            1.1.2.2.2.1.1. Con:
            Some adopted children tend to be particularly vulnerable [13], and so there may be a need to have a higher standard for prospective adoptive parents than for biological parents.

              1.1.2.2.2.1.1.1. Con:
              If this is the case, then the standard being applied to all parents under a licensing system would be higher, thus leading to better parenting.

              1.1.2.2.2.1.1.2. Pro:
              Children who are adopted from other countries would take longer to adapt to a new language and culture [14], and as a result, may require parents with extra training or time.

              1.1.2.2.2.1.1.3. Pro:
              Many children who are in need of adoptive families have come from abusive homes. This can result in various developmental issues [15], which would require their care to encorprate additional support and expertise.

            1.1.2.2.2.1.2. Pro:
            Foster carers are subject to regular reviews [16], after they have been approved, to ensure the required criteria are upheld in the long-run.

              1.1.2.2.2.1.2.1. Con:
              According to the Child Advocate Review, the quality of safety assessments only met standards in 46% of reviewed cases (p. 15 [17]).

              1.1.2.2.2.1.2.2. Con:
              Some foster parents are accused of undertaking the role to benefit from the financial stipend [18] that comes with it, rather than to care for the incoming child.

                1.1.2.2.2.1.2.2.1. Con:
                Providing foster parents with financial compensation for their work is simply a way to attribute value to the labour which society traditionally undervalues.

                1.1.2.2.2.1.2.2.2. Pro:
                Some agencies view the stipend offered to foster parents as a fee for a professional [19] service, suggesting that the role is regarded as a way of making money rather than a way of caring for a child in need.

                1.1.2.2.2.1.2.2.3. Pro:
                Caring for children with autism or physical disabilities comes with a higher weekly stipend [20], thereby putting these vulnerable children at further risk of ending up with a foster parent who is 'in it for the money'.

                1.1.2.2.2.1.2.2.4. Con:
                On average, the stipend that foster parents receive is equivalent to $1/hour and does not even cover the child's living expenses. Fostering thus often costs the carers [21] money, rather than earning them any additional income.

              1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3. Pro:
              Prospective foster carers are assessed on their understanding of key issues including sexual boundaries, promotion of education, and approaches to discipline (p. 8 [22]).

                1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3.1. Con:
                There are a number of testimonies [23] from those who have had negative experiences of foster care. The assessment process is therefore far from fool-proof.

                  1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3.1.1. Pro:
                  In the UK [24], 88% of abuse or neglect in foster care is perpetrated by the carers themselves.

                  1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3.1.2. Pro:
                  Private foster care agencies have allowed [25] criminals and registered sex offenders to acquire licenses and adopt foster children. This has led to neglect, sexual abuse, and murders of children.

                    1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3.1.2.1. Pro:

                    1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3.1.2.2. Pro:
                    A caseworker in one foster agency reported that three girls were happy and healthy in their foster home when they had been dead for three months [27]. This is indicative of the ineptitude possible in foster care agencies.

                    1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3.1.2.3. Pro:
                    Many LGBTQ children in foster homes suffer abuse. As a result, in the US, many LGBTQ youth have experienced homelessness [28] because they believed they were safer on the streets than they were in their foster homes.

                    1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3.1.2.4. Con:
                    Many states allow former criminals to adopt, once they have shown evidence of their rehabilitation [29]. The fact that criminals may be allowed to adopt is not, in and of itself, an issue.

                  1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3.1.3. Con:
                  Individual testimonies do not serve to invalidate the entire process.

                1.1.2.2.2.1.2.3.2. Pro:
                Assessors also contact prospective foster carers' previous partners and non-relatives for references [30] so that their personality and capabilities are evidenced.

              1.1.2.2.2.1.2.4. Pro:
              Prospective carers must guarantee that the incoming child will be given appropriate accommodation (pp. 3-4 [31]).

            1.1.2.2.2.1.3. Pro:
            Adopting a child requires a certain set of conditions [32] to be met. Given that society approves of this, there is no reason why we should not impose these conditions on those who wish to procreate.

              1.1.2.2.2.1.3.1. Pro:

              1.1.2.2.2.1.3.2. Con:
              Bureaucratic red tape and imperfect, discriminatory adoption criteria prevent [34] many inviduals from adopting children.

                1.1.2.2.2.1.3.2.1. Con:
                No policy is ever perfectly planned, and submitting its conditions to a standard of perfection is unreasonable.

                1.1.2.2.2.1.3.2.2. Pro:

                1.1.2.2.2.1.3.2.3. Pro:
                Red tape can make the process incredibly daunting [36], and so suitable couples may not even apply to become adoptive parents if they assume they would not be successful.

          1.1.2.2.2.2. Con:
          There is no way to accurately assess a person's parenting capabilities.

            1.1.2.2.2.2.1. Pro:
            Different countries [37] have extremely different parenting styles; what is deemed good in one country may be deemed bad in another. This is further testament to the lack of objectivity in determining what makes for good parenting.

              1.1.2.2.2.2.1.1. Con:
              Just as adoption laws vary by country [38], licensing laws will be decided by individual governments. Thus, licensing policies are likely to be in line with regional cultural standards and expectations of good parenting.

                1.1.2.2.2.2.1.1.1. Con:
                Not all cultural groups within one country have similar standards of parenting. Some groups, for example, do not have biological parents as primary caregivers for their children.

                  1.1.2.2.2.2.1.1.1.1. Pro:
                  For example, Aboriginal Australians have a complex kinship system [39] which is only loosely related to biological lineage. This falls outside of the norm of what some cultures would consider 'normal' parenting.

                  1.1.2.2.2.2.1.1.1.2. Pro:
                  In the culture of Navajo, for instance, children are not regarded as children of the parents but rather children of the clan (p. 611 [40]).

              1.1.2.2.2.2.1.2. Pro:
              While in Korea the traditional approach of cultivating obedience in children remains popular, Scandinavian parents prefer [41] building a more democratic relationship with their children.

              1.1.2.2.2.2.1.3. Pro:
              Different parts of the world [42], and different racial or ethnic groups, have differing opinions on the merits of corporal punishment [43].

                1.1.2.2.2.2.1.3.1. Con:
                Findings from strong, empirical analyses [44] document adverse outcomes from physical discipline use, including spanking.

                1.1.2.2.2.2.1.3.2. Pro:
                Some countries have made spanking illegal [45], while it is a part of other cultures [46].

            1.1.2.2.2.2.2. Pro:
            The most important metrics for such a test would be subjective, which means they cannot be accurately assessed.

              1.1.2.2.2.2.2.1. Pro:
              Even if parents engage in the system as honestly as possible, a one time assessment cannot account for the varying levels of parenting stress a parent may face throughout their life, which highly affects [47] their parenting behaviour.

              1.1.2.2.2.2.2.2. Pro:
              The extent to which mental health and mental illnesses impact parenting capabilities varies from person to person.

                1.1.2.2.2.2.2.2.1. Pro:

                1.1.2.2.2.2.2.2.2. Pro:
                Those who have Borderline Personality Disorder (BPD) can be less competent in their parenting, but those with BPD who can subjectively enter the mind of their child are better able to competently parent (p. 12 [49]).

              1.1.2.2.2.2.2.3. Con:
              It is better to assess some things that affect parenting than to assess none. While the licensing policy won't be able to identify all bad prospective parents, it is useful to be able to identify as many as we can.

              1.1.2.2.2.2.2.4. Pro:
              It is impossible to determine whether a parent will be overly-controlling, protective, or pressuring from a test that is likely to depend on self-diagnosis.

              1.1.2.2.2.2.2.5. Pro:
              A parenting license incentivises people to cover up or lie about their flaws, rather than seeking help.

                1.1.2.2.2.2.2.5.1. Con:

                1.1.2.2.2.2.2.5.2. Pro:
                For example, someone who struggles with substance abuse [51] but hopes to one day be a parent may be less likely to engage with the rehabilitation infrastructure if they fear it is likely to be held against them [52] when they apply for a license, as it sometimes is in adoption cases.

                  1.1.2.2.2.2.2.5.2.1. Con:
                  In the European Union, GDPR [53] prevents the sharing of an individual's information for purposes for which they have not consented. Attempts to share information between agencies would therefore contravene this regulation.

            1.1.2.2.2.2.3. Con:

            Children growing up with same-sex parents are at no disadvantage [408] compared to those with heterosexual parents. The licensing body would thus have no objective justification for discriminating against same-sex couples.

            1.5.3.4.1.1.2. Con:
            In 2017, Texas [409] passed a bill that allowed state-funded adoption agencies to deny adoption [410] to those belonging to certain groups - including LGBTQ+ couples - based on their own religious views.

            1.5.3.4.1.1.3. Con:
            Countries other than the US do ban or restrict gay adoption [411].

              1.5.3.4.1.1.3.1. Pro:
              In Israel [412], LGBTQ+ couples applying to adopt are explicitly discriminated against. Such discrimination could be replicated under the parenting licensing model.

          1.5.3.4.1.2. Pro:
          The Catholic Church [413] has long opposed same-sex parenthood. In countries where the religion has a significant standing, it is likely that the license's conditions would be prejudiced against same-sex couples.

        1.5.3.4.2. Con:
        Current proposed ammendments to US law would extended [414] anti-discrimination laws to protect prospective adoptive and foster parents who risk prejudice in this way. The same could be done in the case of parental licenses.

        1.5.3.4.3. Pro:
        Licensing could discriminate against single parents.

          1.5.3.4.3.1. Con:
          Children of single parents are at a disadvantage [415]; therefore creating a policy resulting in fewer single people becoming parents would be justified.

            1.5.3.4.3.1.1. Con:
            While the children of single parents may be more at-risk, that does not mean they cannot overcome the challenges associated with it and grow and thrive.

          1.5.3.4.3.2. Con:
          Many countries allow single parents to adopt.

            1.5.3.4.3.2.1. Pro:
            In the US, a prospective parent's marital status or relationship status does not affect [416] their eligibility to adopt.

            1.5.3.4.3.2.2. Pro:
            There are no adoption restrictions based on the prospective parent's partner status [417] in the UK.

            1.5.3.4.3.2.3. Pro:
            Application for adoptions in Australia [418] do not require the prospective parent to be in a relationship.

            1.5.3.4.3.2.4. Con:
            In Germany, single parents can only adopt under 'exceptional cases [419]'.

            1.5.3.4.3.2.5. Con:
            While it is not prohibited, adoption agencies and birth parents often exhibit a bias [420] towards two-parent homes, leaving single parents at the bottom of the waiting list.

              1.5.3.4.3.2.5.1. Pro:
              In Canada, single parents are 50% less likely [421] to be placed with a child.

          1.5.3.4.3.3. Pro:
          A financial requirement would disproportionately affect single parents as they earn less than married couples.

            1.5.3.4.3.3.1. Pro:
            In South Korea [422], single-parent households earn less than half the national average.

            1.5.3.4.3.3.2. Pro:
            A significant portion of single mothers in the US live in poverty [423].

            1.5.3.4.3.3.3. Con:
            Welfare states provide assistance to single parents. This would still exist in a world where parenting licenses exist, allowing single parents to raise children.

              1.5.3.4.3.3.3.1. Pro:
              Australia [424] has a welfare programme which offers payments to single parents.

            1.5.3.4.3.3.4. Pro:
            Single parent families across the developed world are significantly more likely to live in poverty [425] than families with two parents.

              1.5.3.4.3.3.4.1. Pro:
              In 2015, in the US, traditional two-parent families earned an income that was more than three times higher [426] than for households headed by a single mother.

        1.5.3.4.4. Pro:
        -> See 1.2.3.2.

      1.5.3.5. Con:
      Requirements that would become culturally invasive or discriminatory [427] would be open to widespread criticism [428] and backlash, much like discriminatory laws have faced in the past. This makes it less likely that the government would pursue such policies.

        1.5.3.5.1. Con:
        Widespread backlash may not arise if the minority group is not liked [429] by society at large.

        1.5.3.5.2. Pro:
        Attempting to use standardised parenting tests to eradicate a group of people would be quickly noticed, in the case that few members of a minority group acquire licenses.

        1.5.3.5.3. Con:
        Many countries are already committing atrocities [430] against minority groups, with little international accountability.
